What Is A Wetland?

It is an area of land whose soil is permanently or seasonally saturated with water they include streams, swamps, lakes, mudflats, bogs, mangroves and lagoons. The water can be fresh, salty or brackish and water is the most important factor controlling the environment, plant and animal life.

The Environmental Benefits

Wetlands plants act as a filter absorbing solids and adding oxygen to the water. They remove nutrients and act like cleaners which protects the whole waterway from silt and pollutants. Wetlands help with flooding by absorbing heavy rain and releasing it slowly which helps protect shorelines and riverbanks being washed away.

Image credit: Bay of Plenty Council

Wetlands support more bird life compared to a forest area of similar size. Threatened species like the spoonbill, brown teal and white heron depend on what wetlands are left. They are a spawning habitat for native fish and the decline of native fish is directly linked to the loss of wetlands.

What are threats to Wetlands?

  • Farm animals grazing in wetlands damages vegetation and destabilises the soil.
  • Wetlands are reduced because of reclamation of land around lakes, estuaries and draining of farm swamps.
  • Recreational boating and jet skiing disturbs plants and animal life and may destroy parts of the wetland environment.
  • Animal and plant pests invading wetlands and whitebait fishing.
  • Removing sand and gravel causes a change in water levels which damages vegetation and weeds can enter and establish themselves.
